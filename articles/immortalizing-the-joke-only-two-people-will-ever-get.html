Immortalizing the Joke Only Two People Will Ever Get

Every close friendship eventually develops at least one joke that, if you tried to explain it to a stranger, would take fifteen minutes, require three separate flashbacks, and still land with the explanatory power of a shrug. You know the one. Maybe it's a word that means something only to you two now. Maybe it's a single photo that makes you both lose it instantly, for reasons nobody outside the friendship could reconstruct.

Linguists who study in-group language (sometimes called "relational idiom" in sociolinguistics research) have found that these private references actually serve a real social function — they're shorthand proof of shared history, and the more obscure and specific the joke, the stronger the bond it signals. In other words: the dumber and more inexplicable the joke, the more it actually means.

The Tragedy of the Disappearing Inside Joke

Here's the problem. Inside jokes live almost entirely in memory and group chats, both of which are unreliable long-term storage. The group chat gets buried under thousands of newer messages. Phones get upgraded and chat history doesn't always transfer cleanly. And memory, as covered extensively in psychology research on memory decay, gets less precise every year — eventually you remember that the joke was funny without remembering why.

This means most inside jokes are, on a long enough timeline, doomed. Which is sad, because they're often the single funniest artifact of an entire friendship.

Ways People Try (and Mostly Fail) to Preserve Inside Jokes

  • Screenshotting the original message (lost the next time you clean out your camera roll)
  • Making it your relationship's running gift theme (works until someone forgets the reference entirely)
  • Telling the story at every gathering until everyone's sick of it (effective but exhausting)
  • Getting matching merch with the joke on it (commits you to wearing it in public, forever)

A Genuinely Permanent Option

This is, somewhat absurdly, exactly what some friend groups use a GalaxySpace star for — naming a star after the joke itself, with the full ridiculous context written into the message field, so it exists somewhere permanent that doesn't depend on anyone's camera roll surviving a phone upgrade.

Is naming a star after the time your friend said "that's not a real owl" with complete confidence at a zoo slightly unhinged? Sure. Is it also the most honest, accurate monument to that specific friendship? Also yes.
