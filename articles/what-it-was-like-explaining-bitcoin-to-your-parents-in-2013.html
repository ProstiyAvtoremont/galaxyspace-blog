What It Was Like Explaining Bitcoin to Your Parents in 2013

Anyone who got into crypto early enough has some version of the same story: trying to explain Bitcoin, or later Dogecoin, to a parent or older relative, and watching their face shift from confusion to polite concern to, in some memorable cases, outright alarm that their kid had apparently been talked into some kind of internet scam.

Why This Memory Sticks for So Many Early Adopters

These conversations tended to follow a predictable, slightly painful arc: the explanation of "it's digital money that isn't controlled by a bank," the inevitable "but who actually backs it?" question, and the conversation usually ending somewhere between "that sounds risky" and "please be careful." For people who were genuinely excited about something new, this kind of well-meaning skepticism from family could feel oddly lonely, even when it came from a place of care.

Psychologists studying belief and social validation note that humans have a strong drive to share enthusiasm and have it mirrored back — when that mirroring doesn't happen, especially from people whose opinion matters, the experience tends to imprint more strongly than if a stranger had reacted the same way. This is likely part of why so many early crypto adopters can recall these specific family conversations in vivid detail, even a decade or more later.

What Changed, and What Didn't

A decade on, many of these same family members have entirely different views on crypto — some have come around, some still haven't, and plenty of early adopters have, with some satisfaction, gotten to revisit those old conversations from a very different position. Regardless of how it played out financially, the social experience of being an early, slightly isolated believer in something most people around you found bizarre is its own specific, formative memory.
