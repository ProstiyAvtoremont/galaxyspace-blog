The People Who Survived Your Dorm Years With You

There is no relationship quite like the college roommate relationship. You go from total strangers to sharing a space roughly the size of a parking spot, including all the associated chaos: the 3 a.m. conversations about nothing, the shared mini-fridge negotiations, the time one of you brought home a truly terrible decision at 2 a.m. and the other one had to deal with the consequences by sunrise.

Sociologists studying college friendships have noted something specific about roommate bonds: the forced proximity creates an unusually fast, unusually intense form of closeness — researchers sometimes call this the "mere exposure effect" combined with shared adversity, which is academic language for "you saw each other at your absolute worst within the first semester and somehow became close anyway."

The Specific, Universal Roommate Experience

If you had a good college roommate, this list will hit uncomfortably close to home:

  • They've seen you cry over a grade, a breakup, and a vending machine eating your last $3, sometimes all in the same week
  • They know your actual sleep schedule, which is to say, they know you have none
  • They've covered for you with an RA at least once, no questions asked
  • You went from "we don't know each other" to "this person knows things about me my own family doesn't" in roughly one semester

Why These Bonds Often Outlast Everything Else From College

A surprising number of people stay closer with their college roommates than with people from their actual hometown or major — not because the roommate relationship was necessarily the "best" friendship of college, but because it was forged under such specific, weird, intense conditions that nothing else from that era quite compares.
