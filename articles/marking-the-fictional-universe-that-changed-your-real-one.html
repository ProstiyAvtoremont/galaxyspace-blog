Marking the Fictional Universe That Changed Your Real One

Everyone has at least one fictional universe that did something a little embarrassing to their actual real life — shaped their sense of humor, gave them a friend group, possibly influenced a career choice, or at minimum produced an inside-joke vocabulary that's completely unintelligible to anyone outside the fandom.

This isn't a niche phenomenon limited to a few intense hobbyists. It's a near-universal experience that people just don't always admit to with a straight face.

Why Fandoms Have This Much Actual Influence

Media psychologists studying "parasocial" engagement (the real emotional relationships people form with fictional characters and worlds) have found that deep engagement with a fictional universe activates many of the same social and emotional brain processes as real relationships and communities. This isn't a deficiency or a substitute for "real" connection — fandoms regularly produce genuinely real friendships, communities, and even marriages between people who met purely through shared love of the same fictional world.

The Specific, Slightly Unhinged Things Fandoms Produce

  • An entire shared vocabulary that means nothing outside the group
  • Friendships that started purely over a shared opinion about a fictional character's choices
  • A genuinely emotional reaction to a fictional death that outlasts emotional reactions to plenty of real news
  • Conventions, fan fiction, fan art, and entire economies of creative output built purely on love for something that, technically, isn't real

Why This Deserves to Be Taken at Least Half-Seriously

The friendships and communities that fandoms produce are completely real, even though their origin point is fictional. Treating that as embarrassing rather than meaningful misses what's actually going on — a shared fictional universe is just as legitimate a foundation for community as a shared hometown or shared profession, arguably more so, since nobody chooses their hometown.

Giving the Fandom a Permanent, Slightly Silly Monument

Some fan communities have started marking this with a shared star on GalaxySpace — named after the fictional universe itself, with a message about what it actually meant, written by people who'd understand exactly why it matters even if nobody else would.

You can create one here — a permanent, completely earned tribute to whatever fictional world quietly shaped your very real life.
