Why Kids Ask "How Big Is Space?" and What to Tell Them

Somewhere around age five or six, most kids ask a version of the same impossible question: how big is space, actually? It usually arrives at an inconvenient time — bedtime, the car, the middle of an unrelated conversation about dinosaurs — and most parents respond with some version of "really, really big," which is true but not actually satisfying to anyone, including the parent who just said it.

Here's how to actually answer it, step by step, in a way that holds up to a kid's follow-up questions (and there will be follow-up questions).

Step 1: Start With Something They Can Picture

Don't start with light-years. Start with something concrete: the Earth could fit inside the Sun more than a million times over. Most kids find this immediately, viscerally interesting, because it's a number they can sort of picture even if they can't fully grasp it.

Step 2: Introduce Distance Through Time, Not Numbers

"Light from the Sun takes about 8 minutes to reach us" lands better with kids than any distance measurement in miles. From there: light from the nearest star beyond our Sun takes over four years to arrive. Some of the light hitting their eyes tonight left a star before they were even born.

Step 3: Let the Question Stay a Little Unanswerable

Child development researchers studying curiosity and cognitive development have found that kids actually benefit from questions that don't get a fully tidy answer — it builds tolerance for genuine mystery and curiosity, rather than training them to expect every question to have a quick, complete resolution. It's okay to say "we don't fully know, and that's part of what makes it amazing."

Step 4: Give Them Something to Hold Onto

Abstract vastness can occasionally tip from wonder into low-level existential unease, even in young kids (the "are we just tiny specks" spiral is real and surprisingly common). Counter it with something tangible — a specific star they can find in the sky, a specific fact that makes the universe feel less like an overwhelming void and more like a place with structure and stories in it.

Step 5: Make Space Personal, Not Just Vast

This is where a surprising number of parents have found that naming a star together — through something like GalaxySpace — actually helps. It turns an abstract, occasionally overwhelming concept into something specific: "that's your star, with your name on it," which gives a kid a tiny, personal foothold in a universe that otherwise just sounds enormous and a little scary.
