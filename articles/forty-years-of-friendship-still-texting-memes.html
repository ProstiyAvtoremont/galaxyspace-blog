"Forty Years of Friendship, Still Texting Memes"

There's something quietly remarkable about a friendship that's survived forty years — long enough to span multiple decades of completely different technology, completely different life stages, and probably several long stretches of living in different cities, and somehow it's still going, often expressed today through nothing more dramatic than sending each other memes.

Why So Few Friendships Actually Make It This Far

Sociologists studying friendship longevity note that most friendships, even close ones, naturally dissolve over major life transitions — moving, marriage, career changes, kids — unless both people actively maintain the connection through those transitions. A friendship surviving forty years isn't an accident; it's the cumulative result of dozens of small decisions, over decades, by both people, to keep showing up for each other even as life circumstances repeatedly changed around them.

What Decades-Long Friends Actually Have That Newer Friends Don't

It's not just shared history, though that matters. Long-term friendship researchers point to something more specific: an unusually deep, calibrated understanding of how the other person thinks, reacts, and communicates — built from literal decades of data that no newer relationship, however close, can replicate quickly. This is part of why old friends can communicate so much with so little effort; a single meme, with zero explanation, can land perfectly because forty years of context is already built in.

Why These Friendships Rarely Get Properly Marked

Despite being genuinely rare and remarkable, decades-long friendships almost never get any kind of formal acknowledgment, the way long marriages get anniversary parties. There's no cultural script for celebrating "forty years of friendship" the way there is for "fifty years of marriage," even though the achievement, statistically, is just as rare.
