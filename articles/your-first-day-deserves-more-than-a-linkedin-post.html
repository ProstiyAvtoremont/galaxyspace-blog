Your First Day Deserves More Than a LinkedIn Post

You know the post. "Excited to announce that I'll be joining [Company] as [Title]!" Forty-three likes, a handful of "congrats!!" comments from people you went to college with, and then it's gone — buried under everyone else's announcements within about 48 hours, the algorithmic equivalent of confetti that gets swept up the morning after.

This isn't a knock on LinkedIn posts. They serve a real purpose (networking, visibility, professional context). They're just not built to hold onto the actual feeling of a new beginning, which is a different thing entirely from announcing it.

What the LinkedIn Post Doesn't Capture

The post captures the title and the company. It doesn't capture: how nervous you were walking in, what you told yourself in the car to calm down, the genuinely random detail that made the first day feel real (the smell of the break room coffee, a coworker who said one welcoming thing that mattered more than it should have).

Career psychologists studying job transitions have found that the emotional texture of a "first day" — the nerves, the hope, the specific quality of starting over — fades remarkably fast once the job becomes routine, typically within the first few months, even though the title and company name stick around indefinitely on a resume.

Why the Beginning Is Worth Marking Separately From the Title

Five years into a career, almost nobody remembers their exact start date or what their LinkedIn announcement said. What people do remember, when prompted, is the feeling of starting — and that feeling is worth something independent of whether the job itself worked out, got promoted past, or eventually ended in a layoff or a resignation.

A Quieter, More Permanent First-Day Record
