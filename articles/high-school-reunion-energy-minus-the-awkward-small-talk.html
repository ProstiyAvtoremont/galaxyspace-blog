High School Reunion Energy, Minus the Awkward Small Talk

Reunions occupy a strange emotional zone: equal parts genuine excitement and low-grade dread, usually starting around the moment you realize you'll have to answer "so what are you up to now?" approximately forty separate times in one evening.

Here's how to get more of the good part (real reconnection) and less of the bad part (forced small talk with someone you haven't spoken to since a shared locker in 2009).

5 Ways to Actually Enjoy a Reunion

  1. Find your actual people first, not everyone at once. Social psychologists studying group dynamics note that people feel more authentically social in clusters of 2-4, not large mixed groups — track down the two or three people you genuinely want to talk to before getting swept into the general crowd.
  2. Skip the status update, ask the real question. "What are you up to" invites a resume answer. "What's actually been good lately?" invites an actual conversation.
  3. Bring up a specific shared memory immediately. Specificity ("remember when the fire alarm went off during the assembly") cuts through fifteen years of small talk faster than any general catch-up could.
  4. Accept that some friendships were time-and-place, and that's fine. Not every high school friendship is meant to survive into your forties, and reunion night isn't where that gets decided either way.
  5. Document the good parts before they fade, because reunion nostalgia has a notoriously short shelf life — the warm feeling tends to evaporate by the following Tuesday, taking most of the specific memories with it.

Making the Reconnection Last Past Tuesday

That last point is where some reunion attendees have started creating a shared star on GalaxySpace with old friend groups — naming it after the graduating class, the old friend group's nickname, or an inside joke that somehow survived fifteen years apart.

It's a small, slightly absurd, genuinely sweet way to bottle the one good night of reconnecting before everyone scatters back to their separate lives again for another decade. You can create one for your group here — proof the reunion happened, long after the small talk's been forgotten.
